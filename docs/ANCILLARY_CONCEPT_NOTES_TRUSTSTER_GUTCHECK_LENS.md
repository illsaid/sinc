# Sinc Ancillary Concept Notes — Trustster, Gutcheck, Lens

## Purpose
Capture the useful primitives from three adjacent concepts and separate them from the parts that should likely be discarded.

The goal is not to preserve every product pitch.
It is to cull aggressively and retain only what may be useful as:
- a standalone wedge
- an ancillary Sinc product
- a feature family inside the broader Sinc universe

---

# 1. Trustster

## Overall assessment
Mostly not worth pursuing as a standalone product in current form.

The concept repeatedly drifts into:
- universal reputation logic
- graph gamification
- portable trust scores
- growth loops and score inflation
- decentralized-solutionist garnish

Those moves corrupt the most interesting primitive.

## Keeper primitives
### A. Signed, scoped attestations
Portable, domain-specific, revocable attestations remain interesting.

Examples:
- “I trust this person’s judgment on Japanese cinema.”
- “I have worked with this person as a freelance editor and would hire them again.”

This is useful when treated as:
- bounded
- contextual
- inspectable
- non-scalar

### B. Visible provenance paths
Knowing who vouched for whom, and through what trust path, is useful.

For Sinc this matters less as a global graph and more as:
- “why is this shelf or recommendation reaching me?”
- “whose judgment stands behind this?”

### C. Revocation / tombstones
The idea that trust statements should be withdrawable without disappearing from history is valuable.

This matters more for integrity than for public permanence.

## What to discard
- global trust scores
- mutual-vouch boosts
- viral loops
- public graphification
- platform/API embedding of scores
- blockchain/permanence rhetoric
- “Napster for trust” as literal full-system ambition
- any marketplace logic based on human trust scores

## Best use inside Sinc
Not as a universal reputation layer.
Use only as a quiet attestation/provenance primitive:
- domain-specific trust receipts
- visible one-hop provenance
- revocable vouching

Useful phrase:
Portable reputation is dangerous.
Portable attestation is interesting.

# 2. Gutcheck

## Overall assessment
Most promising of the three as an ancillary wedge.

Gutcheck has a real product bone:
- a lightweight judgment overlay for the live web
- extension-first
- tiny glanceable signals from trusted people
- low friction
- in situ emotional relief

This is much closer to an actually usable prosthesis than Trustster.

## Keeper primitives
### A. Browser overlay for trusted human judgment
Strong concept.

A lightweight layer on top of the existing web that answers:
- worth it
- skip it
- sketchy
- gold

without requiring a new destination product.

This is a highly plausible wedge.

### B. Topic-specific trust circles
Very strong.

Not:
- “I trust Maya” globally

But:
- “I trust Maya on biotech”
- “I trust Eli on film”
- “I trust Roma on household aesthetics”

This is aligned with Sinc’s best instincts.

### C. On-demand judgment query
“Gutcheck this” is a very good interaction primitive.

Paste a link or activate on-page and ask:
- what do my trusted people think?
- can I safely skip this?

### D. Short in-context reasons
Not just a colored badge.
A tiny visible reason matters.

Examples:
- “Redundant with yesterday’s Atlantic piece.”
- “Worth it for section 3 only.”
- “Bad-faith framing.”

## Risks / constraints
- can become another social-proof layer if overdone
- can become a bubble amplifier if disagreement is hidden
- can recreate a feed if “what my people endorse” grows into another scrolling surface
- can be corrupted by propagation/amplification metrics

## What to discard
- trustster dependency/integration
- mutual-trust boosts
- priority propagation
- publisher-facing monetization from judgment exhaust
- analytics that encourage tribal optimization more than discernment
- blockchain/P2P as user-facing religion

## Best use inside Sinc
This is a strong candidate for:
- extension wedge
- ambient judgment layer
- “live web” companion to shelf-based Sinc

Possible role:
- Sinc = shelf / list / curation substrate
- Gutcheck = low-friction judgment overlay for browsing the raw web

Useful phrase:
Human judgment overlay for the live web.

# 3. Lens

## Overall assessment
Interesting, but weaker than Gutcheck as a first wedge.

Lens starts from the same promising surface area as Gutcheck, but moves toward richer synthesis and contextual mediation.
That can be valuable, but it increases contribution burden and risks overbuilding too early.

## Keeper primitives
### A. Consensus + divergence view
Very good.

Not just:
- “your people like this”

But:
- “most of your trusted people think this is worth attention, but two flag a major omission”

This creates a more adult and less tribal form of trusted mediation.

### B. Structured mediation tags
Useful tags include:
- accurate
- overhyped
- biased
- misleading
- missing context
- important

This is a good middle ground between raw thumbs and full essay.

### C. Before-you-engage brief
A concise trusted summary before the user spends attention/emotion is valuable, especially for:
- controversial topics
- long or technical pieces
- manipulative headlines
- high-stakes domains

### D. Richer mode for high-stakes objects
Lens is probably best interpreted not as a separate primary app, but as a deeper mode that activates when:
- there is enough signal from trusted people
- the object is important or contested
- quick verdicts are not enough

## What to discard or defer
- trustster integration
- heavy synthesis as the default everywhere
- publisher/creator monetization based on user mediation exhaust
- “verified Lens” badges
- search-engine replacement ambitions too early
- voice notes as core mechanic unless clearly proven useful

## Best use inside Sinc
Lens feels strongest as:
- a deeper interpretive layer above Gutcheck
- a high-context mode inside Sinc for ambiguous/high-stakes material

Possible relationship:
- Gutcheck = fast reflex
- Lens = considered interpretation

This makes sense as a feature family rather than a standalone first product.

# Cross-concept synthesis

## Most useful primitives overall
### 1. Domain-specific trust
This keeps recurring for a reason.
It is one of the strongest and most reusable concepts.

### 2. Provenance visibility
Users should be able to see why an item or judgment is reaching them.

### 3. Negative curation
Short reasons for passing, dismissing, or flagging redundancy may be more valuable than another save.

### 4. Lightweight in-situ mediation
The live-web overlay concept is one of the strongest adjacent wedges discovered so far.

### 5. Consensus + divergence
Human judgment should not collapse into a binary tribe signal.
Visible disagreement matters.

## Most likely product roles
### Best standalone ancillary wedge
Gutcheck

### Best richer feature layer
Lens

### Best primitive, not product
Trustster’s attestation/provenance components

## Suggested relationship to the Sinc universe
- Sinc core: finite shelves, routing, completion, borrowed taste, deliberate refill
- Gutcheck layer: ambient overlay on the live web for quick trusted judgments
- Lens layer: deeper structured mediation for high-stakes or ambiguous objects
- Trust/attestation layer: quiet domain-specific vouching + provenance paths, if ever needed

## Compressed conclusion
The strongest salvage from these three ideas is not a decentralized trust economy.
It is a lightweight, domain-specific human judgment layer that can sit on top of the live web, with visible provenance, short reasons, and optional deeper context when needed.

That is worth keeping close to the main Sinc line.
